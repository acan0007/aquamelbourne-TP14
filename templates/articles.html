Water quality in Port Phillip Bay still recovering after Victoria's record floods

Water quality in Port Phillip Bay has hit a near-decade low in the wake of record rainfall, but is quickly bouncing back, environmental authorities say.

Water quality in Port Phillip Bay has hit a near-decade low in the wake of record rainfall, but is quickly bouncing back, environmental authorities say.

Key points:
• The drop in water quality has been caused by record rainfall in October and November
• The EPA said there was anecdotal evidence of some sickness linked to the poor water quality but the bay is already making a quick recovery as the weather improves
• Elevated amounts of trace metals, pesticides and E. coli were detected in the bay in the latest water quality reporting period, the Environment Protection Authority (EPA) said on Wednesday.
• Between 2013 and 2021, water quality in December met environmental standards around 90 per cent of the time across 36 bayside beaches monitored by the EPA but that plunged to around 77 per cent in December 2022.
• "It's actually worse than what it's been for previous years," EPA Victoria chief environmental scientist Mark Patrick Taylor said.
• "All the rain in late spring and early summer have flushed contaminants from streams and parks and roadways into the water system," he said.

Melbourne suburbs served by Yarra Valley Water, South East Water warned to boil water after wild weather

Hundreds of thousands of households across Melbourne's north and east have been told to boil their water after storms on Thursday caused electrical damage which resulted in contamination.

Hundreds of thousands of households across Melbourne's north and east have been told to boil their water after storms on Thursday caused electrical damage which resulted in contamination.

Key points:
• Yarra Valley Water advised residents of 88 suburbs to boil their water
• South East Water later identified another 11 affected suburbs
• The companies said the risk of illness was low
• Wild weather including winds of up to 124 kilometres per hour caused widespread damage, and falling trees claimed the lives of three people.

• The storm also damaged infrastructure, allowing undisinfected water from Silvan Dam, east of Melbourne, to enter the water supply system.

• "We've become aware of a potential water quality issue affecting 88 suburbs due to electrical damage caused by storms overnight," Yarra Valley Water said on its Facebook page.

• South East Water later identified another 11 suburbs where residents should boil their water before drinking it as a precautionary measure.

• "Residents should bring water to a rolling boil before drinking, washing and preparing food, preparing baby formula, brushing teeth and making ice until further notice," South East Water said on Facebook.

• Yarra Valley Water said: "Some water may have entered our drinking water supply network that had not been treated to the usual standard."

About the Environment Reference Standard

The Environment Reference Standard (PDF 987KB) is part of the Environment Protection Act 2017 and commenced on 1 July 2021.

The ERS: identifies environmental values that the Victorian community want to achieve and maintain provides a way to assess those environmental values in locations across Victoria. About environmental values

An environmental value is an aspect of the environment and how we use it that is important to us. It is an outcome Victorians want for human health and the environment.

Examples of environmental values include:

• Clean water to swim in • Sound levels that let us sleep at the night • Air quality that supports human health and the environment.
What the Environment Reference Standard covers
• The ERS is itself made up of many reference standards.

These reference standards are in groups that cover four aspects of Victoriaâ€™s environment:

• Ambient air
• Ambient sound
• Land
• Water (surface water and groundwater).

Each reference standard identifies an environmental value.

Most reference standards also have indicators and objectives. These are how we assess if the environmental value is being achieved, maintained or threatened.

An indicator is something we measure. For example, carbon monoxide is one indicator we use to measure ambient air quality.

The ERS also defines the parts of Victoria that the reference standards relate to.

Some standards apply broadly. For example, the ambient air standards relate to all of Victoriaâ€™s ambient air environment. Other standards have environmental values or objectives that just apply to certain areas.

Water pollution

All of the sewage from your house will eventually make its way back into the rivers, lakes, and the sea. Did you or any other members of your family read a newspaper, use packaged products or eat some fresh fruit? All of these things, along with most other things that you use or eat, also produce sewage in their production.

Water pollution is caused by various sources, including untreated sewage and waste entering the environment.
Key contributors include homes, industries, farms, and stormwater drains. Pollutants such as E. coli, toxic
chemicals, oil, and plastics have devastating effects on ecosystems and human health. Effective sewage treatment and proper waste disposal are crucial for mitigating these risks.

Key Points:
• Homes: Improper disposal of hygiene products and chemicals.
• Industries: Toxic waste from chemicals.
• Stormwater: Litter and pollutants from streets.
• Farms: Fertilizer runoff into waterways.

Community Perceptions of Water in Melbourne in 2019

Raw survey data which provides community perceptions of water supply, waterways, flooding management, sewerage services, and the water industry in Melbourne, Australia.

The Community Perceptions of Water in Melbourne (2019) dataset provides insights into how residents view
water-related issues, including water supply, flood management, and sewerage services. It compiles data
from surveys to understand community concerns and satisfaction regarding water management. The dataset
is a valuable tool for assessing public opinion and helping authorities improve water infrastructure and
environmental practices.

Key Points:

• Focuses on public opinions of water supply, flooding, and sewage.
• Highlights concerns about water management efficiency.
• Supports data-driven decisions for future water projects.
